# Reference: profile-evaluation-criteria

## Summary

How to assess a LinkedIn profile for fit, credibility, activity, and outreach potential. Used when summarizing a profile before deciding to pursue, contact, or skip a person.

---

## Key Facts

- The headline is the single most important field on a LinkedIn profile. It is written by the person to describe their professional identity and is the first thing visible in search results.
- A career trajectory is more predictive of a person's trajectory than their current title alone.
- Activity recency (posting, commenting) is the most reliable proxy for LinkedIn engagement and likely responsiveness.
- Mutual connections are the single highest-value signal for accessibility. A warm introduction path is worth more than all other signals combined.
- Sparse profiles are not automatically low-value. Some excellent professionals maintain minimal LinkedIn presence.

---

## Detailed Notes

### The headline

Read the headline carefully. It is chosen by the person to represent their professional identity. Look for:
- **Specificity:** Does it describe what they actually do, or is it a generic title?
- **Domain signals:** Does it mention an industry, specialty, or company?
- **Status signals:** Does it say "Seeking new opportunities" or "Open to work"? (Different context signal)
- **Personal brand elements:** Recruiters often use their headline to signal what they recruit for, which is highly useful targeting information.

### Career trajectory analysis

Read the experience section chronologically (oldest to newest):
- **Direction:** Has this person grown in seniority over time? Stayed flat? Pivoted recently?
- **Domain consistency:** Have they stayed in the same industry/function, or do they have a varied background?
- **Company quality signals:** Have they worked at recognizable or well-regarded companies? (Not gatekeeping — just a signal.)
- **Tenure patterns:** Are they job-hopping (< 1 year average) or stable (2+ years average)? Neither is inherently good or bad, but it informs context.
- **Recent change:** A new role in the last 6 months is a major signal for anyone doing sourcing or networking.

### Activity signals

Check the "Activity" section:
- **Posted original content in last 30 days:** High engagement signal
- **Commented or reacted to posts:** Moderate engagement signal
- **No visible activity:** Either the person hides their activity (privacy setting) or is not active
- **Articles published:** Signals depth of domain thinking; useful for finding personalization hooks

### Skills and endorsements

LinkedIn skills are self-reported and endorsed by connections. They are weak signals for competence but useful for:
- Confirming domain alignment
- Spotting skill-level mismatches with their stated role
- Identifying top endorsed skills that they themselves consider important

### Recommendations

Written recommendations from past colleagues or managers are stronger signals than endorsements. Look for:
- Quality over quantity (one specific recommendation beats five generic ones)
- Who is writing it? (A manager's recommendation outweighs a peer's for seniority assessment)
- Recency (a 10-year-old recommendation has limited current relevance)

### Mutual connections

This is the highest-value signal for accessibility assessment:
- **Strong mutual (1 mutual, you know them well):** Request an introduction before cold outreach
- **Multiple mutuals:** High shared network density — warm path likely
- **No mutuals:** Cold outreach required; lower expected response rate

### Red flags on a profile

- Profile photo is clearly a stock image or AI-generated
- Work history has unexplained years-long gaps without any context
- All skills have maximum endorsement counts (round numbers suggesting manufactured endorsements)
- Very new profile with little history in a specialized domain (potential fake profile)
- Profile says "500+ connections" but has very few followers or engagement (purchased connections pattern)
