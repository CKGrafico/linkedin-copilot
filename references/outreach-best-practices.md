# Reference: outreach-best-practices

## Summary

What makes LinkedIn outreach effective — covering message length, personalization depth, timing, call-to-action design, follow-up cadence, and what consistently causes messages to be ignored or reported.

---

## Key Facts

- Messages under 100 words have higher response rates than messages over 200 words for cold outreach.
- A specific, genuine opening line (referencing something real about the recipient) performs significantly better than a generic opener.
- The best time to message recruiters is Tuesday–Thursday, avoiding Mondays (inbox overload) and Fridays (end of week disengagement).
- One follow-up message, sent 7–10 days after no response, is acceptable. A second follow-up is rarely appropriate and risks being flagged.
- A clear, low-friction call to action (a specific ask that is easy to say yes or no to) outperforms vague openers.
- Personalized connection request notes have higher acceptance rates than blank requests, but only if the note is genuinely personal — not a template.

## Detailed Notes

### Message length norms

| Message type | Recommended length | Maximum practical length |
|---|---|---|
| Connection request note | 1–2 sentences (≤ 200 chars) | 300 characters (platform limit) |
| First direct message (recruiter) | 4–6 sentences | 100–150 words |
| First InMail | 5–7 sentences | 150–200 words |
| Follow-up message | 2–3 sentences | 50–75 words |
| Message to a warm contact | 5–8 sentences | 200 words |

The relationship between message length and response rate is inverse for cold messages: shorter wins. The exception is warm contacts with strong shared context, where more detail can signal genuine effort.

### Personalization depth

**Level 1 (minimum acceptable):** Mentions the recipient's name and current company.
*Not sufficient for cold outreach. This is not personalization, it is mail merge.*

**Level 2 (acceptable):** References their specific role and how it relates to your purpose.
*Better, but still generic if it could apply to many people in the same role.*

**Level 3 (strong):** References something unique to this person — a post they wrote, a career move, a shared connection, a company achievement.
*This is the target for any first cold message.*

**Level 4 (exceptional):** Demonstrates you have engaged with their ideas and have a relevant perspective or connection point.
*Reserved for high-priority targets where the extra effort is justified.*

### Timing

- **Best days to send:** Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- **Worst days to send:** Monday morning (high inbox volume), Friday afternoon (people are mentally checked out)
- **Best time of day:** Mid-morning (9–11am) or early afternoon (1–2pm) in the recipient's time zone
- **After a company announcement:** A congratulatory message immediately after a funding round, launch, or milestone is a natural opening. Act within 48 hours.

### Call to action design

**Effective CTAs:**
- "Would you be open to a 20-minute call in the next couple of weeks?" (specific, time-bounded, easy yes/no)
- "Happy to share my CV if it's useful — or if email is easier: [email]" (low friction, gives them options)
- "I'd love to hear your perspective on [specific topic] — would a brief call work?" (shows you want their input, not just a favor)

**Ineffective CTAs:**
- "I'd love to connect!" (no ask, no direction)
- "Let me know if you have any opportunities" (puts all the work on them)
- "Please review my profile and let me know your thoughts" (demanding, presumptuous)
- "I'd love to pick your brain" (clichéd, implies taking without giving)

### Follow-up cadence

- **First message:** Day 0
- **First follow-up (if no response):** Day 7–10. Keep it brief: 2–3 sentences. Reference your first message. Offer a graceful exit ("Happy to connect another time if now isn't right").
- **No second follow-up** for cold outreach. Move on.
- **Exception:** If you had a prior warm interaction (they liked your post, you met in person), a second follow-up may be appropriate.
